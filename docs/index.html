Revealing the Diversity of Hydropeaking Patterns by Time-Series Data Mining

Tingyu Li¹, Gregory B Pasternack¹

1 Department of Land, Air and Water Resources, Univ. of California at Davis, Davis, CA 95616

1 INTRODUCTION

  • Hydropeaking operation is the rapid variations in power production from hydropower plants due to the varyingelectricity demand.

  • Hydropeaking flow is described as the frequent, large, and rapid flow fluctuations that occur with a certain periodicity.

  • While many studies characterize the diversity of natural flow regimes, the term hydropeaking is used generically for many rivers, without any further detail or analysis as to how there might be systematically different hydropeaking regimes.

2 OBJECTIVES

OBJ1:

Automate hydropeaking events detection and feature extraction to enable data-minig with high temporal and spatial scale data.

 

OBJ2:

Explore the diversity of hydropeaking flow with data mining approaches in California.

Hydrologic features

3 AUTOMATED FEATURE EXTRACTION

To fulfill OBJ1, Hydropeaking Events Detection
Algorithm (HEDA) was developed.

  • To identify change points, flow records are treated as vectors. The change point was tagged with a vector angle larger than 60 degrees.
  • Change points were categorized into four types based on the symbol of the difference between corresponding discharges.
  • To evaluate qualified change points, static and dynamic thresholds that are updated daily were used jointly.

Conceptual diagram of HEDA

4 DATA MINING FRAMEWORK

To fulfill OBJ2, a data mining framework was developed to explain the application of HEDA and statistical explorations of hydropeaking patterns.

  • To have a better performance of HEDA, flow records were split into climatic dry and wet seasons because precipitation or snowmelt can disturb hydropeaking signals.
  • HEDA outputs of the dry-season dataset were used to locate gauges recording hydropeaking flow.
  • Two types of clustering analyses, hierarchical and fuzzy clustering, were conducted to explore data structure with seven independent hydrologic metrics.
  • Clustering analyses were heuristically determined with a combination of statistical interpretation, the examination of hydrographs, and documentation mining.
  • Five major interpretations highlighted in green were made.

5 CALIFORNIA HYDROPEAKING PATTERNS

G2: Large hydropeaking

G2 characterized long-lasting peaking retention. Its peaking phase averagely lasts for five hours. It also features a large variation flow magnitude, usually over two times of annual averaged flow.

 

G2: Frequent hydropeaking

G1 characterized short rise/fall phases of an individual peaking event. G1 has the highest annual frequency, over 80% of the time. Its high annual frequency implies that reduction in magnitudes is compensated by increasing days that peaking is operated.

 

G3: Supplementary hydropeaking

G3 has the highest daily peaking events but with a smaller peaking magnitude and duration. Its relatively low annual frequency might imply that G3 is not responsible for major hydropeaking tasks but to stand by all the time in case of extra electricity generation is needed to maintain the stability of the power grid.

 

G4: Regulated hydropeaking

G4 has the lowest peaking signals. This might be caused by constraints of the environment and facilities. Even though its annual peaking ratio is extremely low (≤41%), the value of other metrics still strongly suggests that hydropeaking operation still exists. This is an interesting group because its weak hydropeaking features are caused either by environmental restriction or the type of dam based on documentation.

6 CONCLUSIONS

  • A new method (HEDA) has been developed in R to automate the feature extraction of hydropeaking with the least subjective decision, adjustment, and iteration and thus allows analysis of hydropeaking flow on a large temporal and spatial scale.

  • Four hydropeaking patterns have been identified: Large (G2), frequent (G1), supplementary (G3), and regulated hydropeaking (G4).

  • Even though this study successfully identified four dominant hydropeaking patterns from raw time-series flow records, one site had a weak membership (<50%) to the assigned group indicating possible transition exists between groups.

  • As for the relative significance of metrics, the duration of the rising/fall process, the number of daily peaking events, and annual frequency are three metrics governing the classification. Additionally, the rate of change surprisingly plays a less important role in classifying hydropeaking patterns than the annual frequency - a metric that is not often used due to the limitations in parameterizing long-term flow records.

  • By analyzing the seasonality of hydropeaking, the variation of hydropeaking patterns between climatic dry and wet seasons was found to be positively related to each other.

Abstract

Operating hydro-electricity generation according to the hourly-adjusted electricity market has been widely applied due to its economic efficiency while also known for its significant impacts to the downstream for producing the sub-daily flow fluctuations called “hydropeaking”. To ascertain the downstream impacts of hydropeaking, features of hydropeaking have been analyzed with biologically-related hydrologic variables. However, studies on hydropeaking are constrained by the manual feature extraction and thus are limited to small temporal and spatial scales. Besides, hydropeaking has been treated as a broadly similar pattern regardless of the complex essence of the electricity market and natural settings. Therefore, this study sought to determine whether there exist significantly different hydropeaking patterns on a regional scale, as revealed by the temporal variation in hydropeaking with a long temporal scale (>five years). To fulfill this goal, a new algorithm, Hydropeaking Event Detection Algorithm (HEDA), was developed in R to automate the parameterization of hydropeaking from public flow records. Using HEDA outputs, classification was conducted to explore the differentiation and similarity of hydropeaking. Four distinct hydropeaking patterns were identified among 33 hydropeaking sites in California. Frequency and duration are two major variables that distinguish the four hydropeaking patterns. Timing of hydropeaking is not exclusively day or night but also includes the uniform and mixed. As for seasonality, hydropeaking mainly occurs in the dry season but tends to occur more frequently in other seasons if hydropeaking is intensive in the dry season.
